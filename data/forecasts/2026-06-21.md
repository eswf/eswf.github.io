## Overview of the Situation

Today's severe weather setup will be driven by a slow-moving stationary front extending from France into Germany, which will gradually shift eastward toward Central Europe. This boundary will act as the primary focus for thunderstorm development, with the most organized and potentially severe storms expected across Germany, where frontal forcing and favorable wind profiles overlap.

As the day progresses, storms are forecast to spread into parts of Czechia and Poland, while a separate corridor of heat-driven convection develops across Slovakia, Hungary, and southeastern Poland. Unlike the frontal storms, these eastern storms will be much more dependent on localized daytime heating, making their exact placement difficult to predict.

The highest confidence for widespread severe weather currently exists within the Moderate Risk (MDT) area stretching from Germany into Czechia and Poland, while two surrounding Enhanced Risk (ENH) regions cover areas where storms may either develop and move into the MDT zone or become severe on their own.

A broader Slight Risk (SLGT) remains in place around the main risk areas, particularly including parts of Austria where conditions appear favorable but model guidance remains inconsistent.

## Synopsis

The MDT area across Germany, Czechia, and Poland features the most favorable overlap of instability, moisture, and wind shear. Soundings indicate moderate to strong instability, with CAPE generally ranging from 1500–3000 J/kg, locally higher in parts of Germany and western Poland. Low-level moisture is abundant, producing relatively low cloud bases and enhancing storm efficiency. Deep-layer shear frequently reaches 35–50 kt, with elongated hodographs supporting organized convection. Several German soundings show strong directional and speed shear through a deep layer, favoring supercells initially, before storms merge into larger clusters or bowing segments along the stationary front. Large hail, severe wind gusts, torrential rainfall, and a few tornadoes appear possible, particularly where storms can remain discrete before upscale growth begins.

The first ENH area, surrounding the MDT zone across Germany, Czechia, and Poland, shows only slightly lower instability but remains strongly supportive of organized storms. CAPE generally ranges between 1000–2500 J/kg, while shear remains sufficient for multicells and transient supercells. Storms forming here may move into the MDT corridor or strengthen locally, producing severe hail, damaging winds, and heavy rainfall.

A second ENH area across Slovakia, Hungary, and southeastern Poland is characterized by a different storm mode. Here, convection is expected to be driven primarily by strong daytime heating rather than frontal forcing. Soundings indicate moderate to locally strong CAPE, often exceeding 2000 J/kg, but with somewhat weaker large-scale forcing. Shear remains adequate for storm organization, meaning that any storm that forms could rapidly become severe. However, coverage will likely be scattered and highly dependent on localized triggers. Large hail and severe downbursts appear to be the primary threats in this region.

The SLGT area, particularly around Graz and southeastern Austria, deserves attention despite limited model support. Soundings suggest sufficient instability and favorable wind profiles for severe storms if initiation occurs. CAPE values are respectable and shear profiles support organized convection. However, because models currently show very little convective development, confidence in storm occurrence remains lower than elsewhere. If storms do manage to form, they could quickly reach severe levels and potentially warrant an upgrade in later outlooks.

Outside these regions, the surrounding MRGL zone contains little indication for widespread convection, and most locations should remain free of significant thunderstorm activity. Overall, the greatest concern remains the frontal severe weather corridor across Germany into Czechia and Poland, where the combination of strong instability, abundant moisture, and favorable shear supports the highest potential for organized severe thunderstorms and possible MCS development later in the day.
